Causal Inference

Problem Domain

Understanding causal relationships in observational data is fundamental to scientific discovery and decision-making. Traditional statistical methods identify correlations but fail to distinguish causation from confounding.

Research Questions

  • How can we identify causal effects from observational data?
  • What computational frameworks enable scalable causal discovery?
  • How do we handle high-dimensional confounders in causal inference?
  • Can we combine domain knowledge with data-driven causal discovery?

Methodology

We develop computational frameworks that combine:

  • Propensity Score Methods: Matching and weighting for treatment effect estimation
  • Instrumental Variables: Leveraging natural experiments for causal identification
  • Double Machine Learning: Combining ML with causal inference for robust estimation
  • Causal Discovery: Structure learning algorithms for causal graph identification

Structural Equation Modelling

Problem Domain

Structural Equation Modelling (SEM) is a powerful statistical technique for testing hypotheses about relationships among observed and latent variables. However, traditional SEM methods exhibit systematic bias in finite samples, leading to incorrect inferences.

Research Questions

  • How can we reduce bias in likelihood ratio tests for SEM?
  • What are the computational methods for Bartlett Correction in SEM?
  • How do we apply SEM bias reduction in healthcare and psychology?
  • Can we develop scalable algorithms for high-dimensional SEM?

Methodology

Our approach focuses on:

  • Bartlett Correction: Analytical and computational methods for bias reduction
  • Computational Statistics: Developing efficient algorithms for SEM estimation
  • Simulation Studies: Rigorous evaluation of bias reduction methods
  • Applied Research: Healthcare, psychology, and social science applications

The bias-corrected likelihood ratio statistic:

$$\Lambda^* = \Lambda \left(1 + \frac{b}{n}\right)$$

where $\Lambda$ is the original LRT statistic, $b$ is the bias correction term, and $n$ is sample size.

Neural-Symbolic Systems

Problem Domain

Deep learning models achieve remarkable performance but lack interpretability and struggle with systematic reasoning. Symbolic AI provides reasoning capabilities but lacks learning from data. Neural-symbolic integration aims to combine the strengths of both paradigms.

Research Questions

  • How can we integrate symbolic reasoning into neural network architectures?
  • What frameworks enable interpretable AI with learning capabilities?
  • How do neural-symbolic systems handle uncertainty and ambiguity?
  • Can we achieve human-like reasoning with neural-symbolic models?

Methodology

Our research combines:

  • Neural Theorem Provers: Combining logic with neural representations
  • Differentiable Programming: Integrating symbolic operations into deep learning
  • Neuro-Symbolic Concept Learners: Learning symbolic concepts from data
  • Compositional Generalization: Testing systematic reasoning capabilities
